Where does the money go?

Unlike other funds that are paid for through tax dollars, the services in the Major Enterprise fund are mostly paid for by user fees.

It is for that reason that this graph looks more symmetrical than the Department of Safety's. Each department here largely pays for itself. The revenue made from fees charged the previous year pay for the expenditures of the current year.

What you should look for is when the number in the left column is lower than the number in the right column. This means that department is spending more money than it raised. When this happens, money is transferred from the General Fund to cover the difference.

In 2019, only the Utilities Fiscal Control spent more than it made.

Glossary

  • Water: To provide a reliable supply of high quality water and customer services to promote public health and safety, economy, and quality of life of Greater Cleveland. [2]
  • Cleveland Public Power: The Division of Water Pollution Control manages the sanitary sewage, stormwater drainage, and combined collections systems in the City of Cleveland. [3]
  • Water Pollution Control: The Division of Water Pollution Control manages the sanitary sewage, stormwater drainage, and combined collections systems in the City of Cleveland. [4]
  • Fiscal Control: To provide professional financial management services and protect the fiscal integrity of funds and assets for all divisions of the City of Cleveland, Department of Public Utilities. [5]
  • Utilities Administration: The Division of Utilities Administration is specifically designed to have administrative charge, control, and supervision over the Divisions of Utilities Fiscal Control, Water, Water Pollution Control, Cleveland Public Power and the Office of Radio Communications. [6]
  • Airport: The Department of Port Control is responsible for the administration and control of all activities at Cleveland Hopkins International and Burke Lakefront Airports as well as the use of City owned land along Lake Erie and the Cuyahoga River. [7]

Clean Water Infrastructure:

Since utilities also include water, we also want to highlight the Cleveland Action Plans water goals. The city has set a goal of having the Cuyahoga River delisted as an area of concern by 2025.

Some of the most aggressive infrastructure projects comprise NEORSD’s Project Clean Lake which aims to capture and treat 98% of all stormwater. This project represents a $3 billion investment over the course of 25 years, with a planned completion in 2036.

Additional projects, such as the restoration of Doan Brook, represent a focus on green infrastructure. This, along with several other projects from local climate groups means the city is on track for hitting this goal.
