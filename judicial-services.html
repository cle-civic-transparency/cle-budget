What is being asked for today?

Expanded Municipal Pretrial Services:

There are a number of ongoing reforms centered on Judicial Services. The primary focuses are bail reform, pretrial services and right to counsel for people facing eviction. What exactly do these terms mean?


Bail reform generally deals with overhauling the bail process. Traditionally, without bail, someone will remain in jail after their arrest until their trial. Bail amounts vary depending on the nature of the offense and act as collateral that ensures the person will show up for their trial.

This system may make sense on the surface but allows for significant disparities because of the already inequitable structure of our society. Low-income, Black and POC individuals are more likely to face difficulties when trying to post bail which can result in additional fallout for their families and dependents.


Pretrial services go hand-in-hand with bail reform. In support of ongoing bail reform, the City of Cleveland created the Pretrial Services Department to assist in supporting individuals prior to their trial dates and aiding in the decision process for the individual - including detention, release, and post-release treatment options and monitoring.


Right-to-counsel is one of the more straightforward terms, meaning that anyone facing an eviction hearing is automatically provided with legal assistance. This type of resource allows for intervention in illegal or unjust evictions that may otherwise fly under the radar.

In 2018, the City of Cleveland appropriated $250,000 to support bail reform and provided another $700,000 to extend the services through 2019. During the first six months when individuals were freed with minimal or no bond, the number of criminal suspects failing to appear for hearings in Cleveland Municipal Court dropped 19%. Cleveland Municipal Court no longer requires bail for non-violent offenders. For all other defendants, including those charged with felonies or violent misdemeanors, a risk-assessment method is used to help determine the likelihood of them committing crimes if released from jail.

Cleveland launched its pretrial services program Sept. 24, 2018, contracting with the nonprofit Oriana House to remind defendants of court dates and monitor them while they await trial. 1,144 defendants participated in this initial program. During this same period, enhanced pretrial services allowed 512 people accused of violent misdemeanors - assault, domestic violence, etc. - to be released with GPS monitoring or supervision. [5]

Tenant's Right to Legal Aid:

In the City of Cleveland, tenants with one or more child living at or below the federal poverty line are entitled to right to counsel when facing eviction. Right to Counsel-Cleveland (RTC-C), a partnership between The Legal Aid Society of Cleveland and United Way of Greater Cleveland, provides this free legal help.

This initiative followed legislation passed by Cleveland City Council in 2019 which make these resources and support a legal right. Cleveland is the fourth city in the nation and the first midwestern city to pass such legislation. [7]
