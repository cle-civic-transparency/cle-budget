Where does the money come from?

Funding for Public Safety comes from The General Fund. The General Fund covers expenses for what we typically think a city does - Mayor, city council, police and more.

Where does the money from and where does it go?

The Department of Public Safety makes up 56.26% of the General Fund budget. The next largest Department is the Department of Public Works, which only takes up 12.13%. The General Fund gets its money from many sources, but most of it (66%) comes from income taxes.

What has been the past history of police reform in Cleveland?

The summer 2020, protests all around America were happening asking for “Defund the Police.” The topic became a national focus, but still has many open questions. What does it mean to defund the police?

Let’s start with a short history of Cleveland police reforms.

2004

In 2004, after years of budget deficits, Cleveland city government was finally forced to make budget cuts to the police department.

$17.5 M cut

2005

More cuts. Note that a decrease in dollars did not mean a decrease in police officers.

$2.8 M cut

+38 employees

2006

Budget cuts did not last.

$5.5 M added

+55 employees

2012

Timothy Russel and Malissa were shot by the police 137 times.

2014

Tamir Rice, an unarmed 12-year-old with a toy gun, was fatally shot by the Cleveland police at close range in the Cudell Commons park, seconds after the police arrived.

2015

In 2015, the Department of Justice issued a consent decree because of constituent excessive use of force. The consent decree had a number of impacts on the Public Safety budget.

Read the Consent Decree

2016

The consent decree changed the budget by allocating new funding to support staffing for the Office of Professional Standards, Police Review Board, Community Police Commission, Police Inspector General, and Department of Justice. The support for these entities added approximately $7.7 M to the Public Safety budget each year, beginning in 2016.

As a result, budgeted staffing levels fluctuated during ensuing years, reaching a low of 1,773 full time employees in 2016 despite a $13.2 M increase from the 2015 budget year. Adjusted for inflation, the 2016 budget year was equivalent to the 2010 funding year, despite the 2010 budget allowing for 90 more full time employees.

$7.7 M added

5 offices added

2020

The murders of George Floyd, Breonna Taylor and other unarmed Black Americans such as locals like Desmond Franklin, ignited widespread protests against police brutality, militarized police forces, and the larger institution of policing in the United States. Black lives matter has been at the forefront of this movement, inspiring widespread demands for cities to “Defund the Police.”

What happened in Cleveland between Tamir Rice and Desmond Franklin?

  • From 2004 to 2015,the Police budget gradually decreased. 2015 marked a 19.2% decrease from 2004 and 20.6% decrease from 2002.
  • However, the five years after 2015 saw a 12.7% increase to the budget with staffing increasing to 1,881 full time employees.
  • How have other safety budgets fared in this same period?
  • Fire has experienced a 23.4% decrease in full time staff since 2002, equating to a loss of 237 full time employees. The budget, adjusted for inflation, has experienced a 17.9% decrease over the same period.
  • EMS has experienced a 17.3% increase in full time staff since 2002, equating to a gain of 49 full time staff. The budget, adjusted for inflation, has experienced a 31.2% increase over the same period.

What reforms are being asked for today?

Here are the organizations working on police reform in Cleveland Black Sprint CLE Demands

1 Year

  • 15% reduction in total tax dollars spent on policing in Cuyahoga County. (Note - This reduction most closely resembles 2012 policing levels, which equate to a 11.2% decrease from 2020. A full 15% decrease would require an $8.2 M reduction from the 2012 levels of policing - an amount in line with what is currently spent on meeting consent decree requirements.)
  • Public commitment from city officials in the 2021 election cycle to establish a pilot program to modify the dispatch system to direct non-police emergency services to non-violent 9-1-1 calls.
  • Establishment of a permanent, elected civilian review board over the Cleveland Police Department with firing power over all officers.
  • Construction of an East Side Trauma Center as paid for by the Cleveland Clinic and University's Hospitals
  • Public comment at all city-council meetings

3 Year
  • 25% Reduction in total tax dollars spent on policing in Cleveland. (This reduction equates to a Police budget of roughly $163.7 M per year - a budget that is still more than four times greater than 2020 funding levels for EMS.)
  • Abolition of at least 3 commissioned police departments in Cuyahoga County
  • Overhaul of dispatch methodologies to exclude police entirely from non-violent emergencies
  • Pilot program established to respond to mental health, domestic violence, or substance abuse related calls
  • Free city-wide WiFi
  • Development of a Resident Community Investment committee for the City of Cleveland overseen exclusively by Cleveland residents

5 Year
  • A single, accountable force under democractic control by the public is responsible for policing in Cuyahoga County.
  • 50% reduction in total tax dollars spent on policing in Cleveland. (This reduction equates to a Police budget of roughly $109 M per year, bringing Police funding in line with what the Fire Department received in 2008 (adjusted for inflation).
  • Decriminalize the possession or use of all drugs in Cuyahoga County, regardless of scheduled status
  • Expunge the records for drug-related possession charges in Cuyahoga County.

  • Solidification of pilot program into county safety budget to respond to mental health, domestic violence, and non-violent emergency calls. This codification shall not increase funding, but shift funding away from the Cleveland Police Department’s annual budget.
  • Non-gated healthcare for all Cleveland residents, including access to abortions and reproductive health care
  • Free Public Transit for all GCRTA buses and trains.
  • Unionized Green Jobs guarantee, regardless of citizenship status
  • Establishment of a Tax Abatement for Public Housing Development for at least 25,000 people.
